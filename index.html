Developmentally Safe Generative AI Environment for Youth

A CHI 2026 workshop on designing, studying, and governing youth-centered generative AI experiences.

Call for Participation Important Dates

Overview

Generative AI (GenAI) systems such as ChatGPT, Gemini, Character AI, and LLaMA are rapidly changing how youth interact with technology. Youth are beginning to view GenAI as creative partners, conversational companions, and sources of emotional connection, introducing new opportunities and challenges for well-being and development.

This workshop brings together researchers, designers, educators, policymakers, and youth-focused practitioners to critically examine how GenAI shapes youths' social, cognitive, emotional, moral, and identity development, and to co-create developmentally safe, inclusive, and empowering GenAI environments.

This workshop builds on the position paper "Developmentally Safe Generative AI Environment for Youth", to appear at the 44th ACM SIGCHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems (CHI 2026), Barcelona, Spain (PDF).

Workshop Goals

The overarching objective of this workshop is to assemble a diverse, interdisciplinary community invested in youth-centered inquiry around generative AI, and to co-develop shared frameworks, methods, and infrastructures that support developmentally safe GenAI ecosystems.

We aim to:

  • Map developmental impacts of GenAI on youths' social, cognitive, emotional, and moral trajectories, including both risks and opportunities.
  • Advance ethical, participatory methods for studying youth–GenAI interactions in-situ, centering youth agency and safety.
  • Identify open challenges and research gaps around youth use of GenAI in real-world settings (home, school, peer, and online spaces).
  • Foster community-based models (e.g., community workshops, longitudinal partnerships) that collaboratively shape GenAI design, education, and policy.
  • Co-create actionable artifacts such as design principles, methodological guidelines, and research agendas for youth-focused GenAI.

Guiding Research Questions

  1. Promising research approaches: What research methods and frameworks are most effective for studying youth interactions with GenAI in-situ? How can we enable ethical, meaningful, and safe data contributions from youth participants?
  2. Open challenges and future directions: Which critical research gaps remain in understanding youth engagement with GenAI systems, and how should we collectively prioritize and address them?
  3. Community-based research models: In what ways can community workshops, longitudinal partnerships, and multi-stakeholder collaborations (families, schools, platforms, policymakers) support developmentally safe GenAI design and governance?

Call for Participation

We invite researchers, practitioners, designers, educators, policymakers, and youth-focused professionals who are interested in the intersections of youth, generative AI, and developmentally informed design.

Topics of interest include (but are not limited to):

  • Youth–GenAI interactions in home, school, peer, and online contexts
  • Developmental risks and opportunities of GenAI (e.g., overtrust, empathy gaps, identity exploration)
  • Bias, safety, and fairness concerns specific to youth users
  • Community-based and participatory research methods with youth and caregivers
  • Design principles for developmentally safe GenAI systems
  • Educational interventions, literacy-building, and co-use practices around GenAI
  • Policy, governance, and multi-stakeholder collaborations for youth GenAI safety

Submission Guidelines

We encourage brief position papers (2–4 pages, excluding references) in the ACM single-column format that describe empirical findings, methodological reflections, design concepts, case studies, or critical perspectives related to the workshop themes.

Submissions will be lightly reviewed for relevance, clarity, and potential to contribute to productive workshop discussion. At least one author of each accepted submission must register for and attend the workshop.

Exact submission details (format, upload site, and deadlines) will follow CHI 2026 workshop guidelines and will be updated here when available.

Background & Resources

The workshop is motivated by emerging evidence that youth engage with GenAI in nuanced and emotionally charged ways, including seeking social support, exploring identity, and developing new forms of trust in AI systems. At the same time, caregivers and educators often have limited visibility into these interactions, and platform safeguards are still evolving.

The position paper and references draw on work in youth online safety, AI risk taxonomies, mental health and conversational AI, and community-based design. For more details, please refer to the manuscript:
